Nature Communications

Emergence of the North Pacific heat storage pattern delayed by decadal wind-driven redistribution

2025DOI: 10.1038/s41467-025-56005-7PMC11733174

Abstract

Storage of anthropogenic heat in the oceans is spatially inhomogeneous, impacting regional climates and human societies. Climate models project enhanced heat storage in the mid-latitude North Pacific (MNP) and much weaker storage in the tropical Pacific. However, the observed heat storage during the past half-century shows a more complex pattern, with limited warming in the MNP and enhanced warming in the northwest tropical Pacific. Here, based on observational datasets, ocean model experiments, and climate models, we show that the emergence of human-induced heat storage is likely postponed in the North Pacific by natural variability until the late-21 century. Specifically, phase shifts of the Pacific Decadal Oscillation have vitally contributed to trends in the North Pacific winds during recent decades. Changes in surface winds drove meridional heat redistribution via Rossby wave dynamics, leading to regional warming and cooling structures and a more complex historical heat storage than models predict. Despite this, enhanced anthropogenic warming has already been emerging in marginal seas along the North Pacific basin rim, for which we shall prepare for the pressing consequences such as increasing marine heatwaves.

Subject terms: Physical oceanography, Physical oceanography, Climate and Earth system modelling, Attribution, Climate-change impacts

Introduction

The storage of excess heat caused by anthropogenic greenhouse warming in oceans is geographically inhomogeneous . Climate models predict that the Southern Ocean , the North Atlantic , and the mid-latitude North Pacific (MNP) will warm up more quickly than other oceans (Fig. ). These ocean warming features accompany accelerated ocean currents , poleward shift of storm tracks and westerlies , increased extreme atmospheric rivers , and altered marine biodiversity patterns . While the enhanced heat storage in the Southern Ocean and North Atlantic has been witnessed in nature, that in the MNP has hardly emerged (Fig. ). Indeed, despite significant warming trends in marginal seas along the basin rim, there were weak warming or cooling trends in the MNP since the 1950s.

The tropical Pacific is among regions with the weakest heat storage in CMIP6 multi-model mean (MMM) (Fig. ). However, the Northwest Tropical Pacific (NWTP) has exhibited the strongest warming rate of the Pacific Ocean during the past decades (Fig. ). Increased ocean heat content (OHC) in the NWTP has led to rapid sea-level rise , increasing marine heatwave and coral bleaching events , and altered tropical cyclone behaviors . It also exerted impacts on the downstream marginal seas through western boundary currents and the Indian Ocean through the Indonesian Throughflow . The Pacific is projected to be the leading reservoir of anthropogenic heat among all oceans by the latter half of the 21 century . Since prediction of climate change relies heavily on the ensemble of climate models, correctly interpreting the discrepancies between MMM and observation represents a vital scientific issue.

Although natural variability exists in individual models, the CMIP6 MMM mainly represents externally forced change . Therefore, the contrasts between observations and CMIP6 MMM indicate either systematic model biases in simulating externally forced heat storage or substantial impacts from natural variability on observed heat storage. Although anthropogenic fingerprints have emerged in many aspects of the ocean , natural variability remains influential in the observed OHC changes . For instance, the persistent negative phase of the Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) since 1998 has led to heat pile-up in the western tropical Pacific via the strengthened Pacific Walker Circulation . Successful attribution of model-observation discrepancies in the North Pacific heat storage to natural variability may enhance our confidence in the projected future change by climate models.

Here, we set out to quantify the contributions of various processes to the observed North Pacific heat storage pattern, addressing in particular whether natural variability can largely explain the model-observation discrepancy. Our analysis is based on (1) five observational datasets to characterize the historical heat storage, (2) experiments of a forced ocean model to isolate the effects of surface heat fluxes and wind-driven ocean dynamics, and (3) 20 CMIP6 models to estimate anthropogenic fingerprints and when they may emerge (Methods). We demonstrate that surface wind changes arising from phase shifts of the PDO have driven basin-scale heat redistribution through Rossby waves and modulations in western boundary currents. This effect complicates the observed heat storage pattern by creating regional warming/cooling structures that conceal anthropogenic fingerprints. According to model projections, the human-induced heat storage pattern will likely hide until the late-21 century. These results provide useful implications for climate prediction in the North Pacific and marginal seas.

Results

Heat storage in the North Pacific

We begin with more details of the simulated and observed heat storage patterns in the North Pacific during the 1958–2021 period. In the MMM of 20 CMIP6 models, consisting of the historical and Shared Socioeconomic Pathway (SSP) 5–8.5 simulations before and after 2014, respectively, the entire MNP is characterized by enhanced 0–2000 m heat storage, in stark contrast to the warming minimum in the western tropical Pacific (Fig. ). The observation-based heat storage shows more regional structures (Fig. and Supplementary Fig. ); for example, there are alternating warming and cooling regions in the North Pacific, particularly in the western basin. The NWTP (e.g., 125°-180°E, 8°–18°N) stands out with a warming maximum, whereas a “warming hole” occurs in the MNP with insignificant trends and is surrounded by significant warming trends in marginal seas, such as the Bering Sea and the Gulf of Alaska. There is another cooling region near the western boundary of the 18°–30°N band, sandwiched by warming areas of the NWTP and the Kuroshio extension (KE) between 30°–40°N. Cooling trends are also seen beyond the North Pacific, such as the subpolar North Atlantic, southwestern subtropical Pacific, and southwestern subtropical Indian Ocean. CMIP6 MMM also shows weakened warming trends in these regions (Fig. ). Albeit with weaker intensity and smaller spatial range, the subpolar North Atlantic warming hole is clearly discernible in CMIP6 MMM. Therefore, models and observations are broadly consonant in all major ocean basins except for the North Pacific. The model-observation discrepancies in the North Pacific heat storage are worthy of in-depth investigation. These discrepancies are also seen in the 0–700 m OHC change (Supplementary Fig. ), which is better covered by historical data in the 20 century than the deep ocean below 700 m.

We further examine the temporal evolution of OHC in key regions (Fig. ). CMIP6 models show an average heat storage rate (i.e., trend of regional average OHC anomalies) of 2.20 ± 2.08 × 10 J m yr (equivalent to 0.71 ± 0.67 W m ; ± indicates the one standard deviation range of 20 models) in the MNP of 170°E-155°W, 40°-52°N, close in magnitude to that of the Southern Ocean (0.66 ± 0.29 W m in MMM for 55°-33°S). In comparison, a much weaker rate of 0.07 ± 0.08 W m is obtained from observation-based datasets for the MNP (Fig. ; ± indicates the one standard deviation range of 5 datasets). Meanwhile, the NWTP shows enhanced warming of 0.58 ± 0.29 W m in observations, one order stronger than the simulated rate of 0.06 ± 0.33 W m in CMIP6 models (Fig. ). Interestingly, CMIP6 agrees with observation in the OHC change of the entire North Pacific (120°E-80°W, 0°-60°N), which are 0.32 ± 0.08 W m and 0.32 ± 0.18 W m , respectively, implying a heat redistribution over the North Pacific in the observed realization relative to the simulated pattern.

The observed OHC anomalies show prominent interannual and decadal variabilities in the MNP and NWTP (Fig. ). Meanwhile, we notice a large inter-model spread relative to the MMM change in the historical simulation of CMIP6 before 2014, which also indicates significant influence from natural variability. The effect of decadal natural variability is particularly notable from the 1990s through the mid-2010s. During this period, the observed OHC of the NWTP was substantially elevated and exceeded the one standard deviation range of CMIP6 models, while that of the MNP did not rise significantly as models expected. Given that anomalies during this period may represent an extreme case of natural climate variability, 20 models are likely insufficient to capture them. Also, we cannot ignore the possibility that CMIP6 models are incapable of reproducing such decadal variability. Due to the significant influence of natural variability, the contrasts between observations and CMIP6 MMM are sensitive to the time length but become stable when the time length exceeds 50 years (Supplementary Fig. ). In the following, we examine how decadal natural variability affects the observed North Pacific heat storage.

Heat redistribution driven by wind changes

To understand how the heat storage pattern is formed, we performed experiments using the Hybrid Coordinate Ocean Model (HYCOM) with a coarse horizontal resolution of 0.5° (Methods) and prescribed reanalysis of atmospheric fields as the surface forcing. Despite regional simulation errors, the control run (CTRL) of HYCOM captured broad-scale features of the observed heat storage—the enhanced warming of the NWTP and the insignificant trends in the MNP ( Fig. ). CTRL also well reproduced the prominent interannual and decadal variabilities (Fig. ). During the simulation period of 1958–2019, the correlation coefficients between CTRL and IAP are 0.44 and 0.81 for OHC anomalies in the MNP and NWTP, respectively, both significant at the 99% confidence level.

With the aid of HYCOM experiments, we can separate the effects of heat redistribution induced by wind-driven ocean circulation changes and heat uptake through surface heat fluxes. The heat-flux run (HTFL) retains changes in surface heat fluxes and keeps wind stress and precipitation invariant (Methods), representing the effect of heat uptake. HTFL produces substantially stronger warming in the MNP than in the tropical Pacific (Fig. ) – a pattern resembling CMIP6 MMM (Fig. ). This highlights the dominance of heat uptake in shaping the model-projected heat storage. Alternatively, the wind stress run (WND), retaining changes only in surface wind stress and keeping other forcing fields unchanged (Methods), produces basin-wide cooling trends in the MNP and enhanced warming of the NWTP (Fig. ). These wind-driven changes greatly modify the pattern shaped by heat uptake and vitally contribute to the total storage in CTRL. Checking the temporal evolution clearly suggests that while heat fluxes drive quasi-monotonic warming trends in both regions (stronger in MNP), interannual and decadal fluctuations arise mainly from winds (stronger in NWTP) (Fig. ). Critically, winds have induced an OHC decrease in the MNP and an abrupt OHC increase in the NWTP since the late 1990s, which greatly altered the overall trends of 1958–2019. The linear separation of the wind and heat flux forcing effects inevitably leads to model errors in HTFL and WND. To assess the potential influence of these artificial errors, we compare the sum of HTFL and WND with CTRL (gray and red lines in Fig. ). The sum of HTFL and WND broadly agrees with CTRL in both OHC changes of the MNP and NWTP (Fig. ) and the heat storage pattern (figure not shown). Despite considerable model errors arising from the linear separation, HTFL and WND still provide useful insights into the underlying processes.

Then, we explore changes in surface winds. Reanalysis datasets suggest basin-scale trends of anti-cyclonic winds over the North Pacific since 1958 (Fig. and Supplementary Fig. ). Correspondingly, there are easterly winds and negative Ekman pumping velocity ω (indicating downwelling; Methods) in the NWTP, which causes convergence of the upper-layer warm water and enhances the heat storage there. Meanwhile, we also see westerly winds and positive w (upwelling) in the eastern tropical Pacific, which might also affect the NWTP through Rossby waves. However, this effect fails to dampen the NWTP warming induced by local wind changes, probably owing to the dissipation of Rossby waves during their transition across the Pacific basin . The anti-cyclone also involves strengthening westerlies north of 40°N, which slackens the heat storage in the subpolar North Pacific through Ekman upwelling and enhances heat pile-up in the 30°-40°N band through Ekman downwelling.

In CMIP6 MMM, trends of anti-cyclonic winds are confined north of 30°N, and westerly trends occupy the entire tropical Pacific (Fig. ), amplifying the model-projected heat storage pattern driven by heat uptake. Some key features of the observed wind trend are missed, including the easterlies in the NWTP and westerlies in the subpolar North Pacific. These differences in surface winds, critically accounting for the model-observation discrepancies in heat storage, likely arise from natural variability, given that the CMIP6 MMM is assumed to represent externally forced changes. The PDO, as the leading mode of decadal natural variability in the North Pacific, was in its positive phase during 1977-1997 and then shifted to its negative phase of 1998–2014 (Supplementary Fig. ). A regression onto the negative PDO index (-PDO) (Fig. ) shows easterlies in the NWTP and westerlies in the northeastern subpolar Pacific – key features seen in observation but missed in CMIP6 MMM, suggesting that the PDO can largely explain the data-model difference in wind trends (Fig. compared to Fig. ). The negative PDO also induces positive w anomalies in the western and central parts of the 18°-30°N band – a feature of obvious data-model discrepancy that explains the absence of the observed cooling trend near the western boundary in CMIP6 MMM (Fig. ).

The regression of OHC onto the negative PDO shows some features resembling the observed heat storage, particularly the warming of the NWTP and the 30°-40°N band and the cooling of the MNP and the 18°-30°N band (Fig. ). The NWTP heat content shows correlation coefficients of -0.42 and -0.66 with the 8-year low-passed PDO index and the unfiltered December-January-February Oceanic Niño Index (ONI), respectively, both significant at the 95% confidence level (Supplementary Fig. ). This suggests a strong modulation of natural variability on the NWTP on interannual and decadal timescales, with heat pile-up in the NWTP under negative PDO and La Niña conditions. In the mid-latitudes, the regression cannot explain the observed heat storage. For example, cooling trends occur mainly in the marginal seas in the regression (Fig. ) rather than in the MNP as in the trend pattern (Fig. ); the meridional dipole-like structure observed east of Japan, linked to the strengthened geostrophic transport of the KE jet (Fig. ), is replaced by prevailing warming in Fig. . These discrepancies can be reconciled by considering delayed response of the ocean to wind forcing . Lagged regressions show that the strengthened KE east of Japan is established ~4 years after the negative peak of PDO, and the MNP cooling takes ~9 years (Fig. ). The wind-driven OHC anomaly of the MNP, as measured by the WND run of HYCOM, also shows a lagged negative correlation with the PDO index (Supplementary Fig. ). The PDO shifted from the negative to the positive phase during the 1970s, and correspondingly there was a warming trend in the MNP throughout the 1980s and 1990s; subsequently, the opposite transition from the late 1990s through ~2010 led to a cooling trend of the MNP persisting through the late 2010s. This lagged response of the MNP region to PDO is also seen in observation (Supplementary Fig. ). The lagged changes to the negative PDO phase are essential in shaping the OHC changes in the MNP and KE regions. Dynamical processes underlying this delayed response demand in-depth understanding, which is the focus of the following subsection.

Regional ocean dynamics

The above analysis points to the vital role of PDO phase shifts in shaping the historical heat storage pattern through wind-driven redistribution. However, correctly simulating the PDO-induced variability, which is critical for predicting regional OHC changes in the upcoming decades and their climatic and environmental impacts, remains a challenging task for state-of-the-art models . Even with prescribed reanalysis winds, our 0.5° simulation of HYCOM fails to fully reproduce the observed heat storage in the MNP (Fig. ); for instance, the simulated cooling in the MNP is stronger in amplitude, broader in spatial extent, and shifted to lower latitudes in CTRL compared to that in observation. These discrepancies probably arise from complex regional ocean dynamics that are not properly represented by coarse-resolution models - such as the “bimodal” variability of the KE .

In the mid-latitudes, a negative phase of the PDO drives downwelling Rossby waves through negative w anomalies between 150°-140°W (Fig. ). Along the latitudinal band of the KE (e.g., 32°-34°N), these Rossby waves propagate across the Pacific basin to the KE region east of Japan by ~4 years, as manifested in satellite-based sea surface height (SSH) anomalies (Fig. ). These downwelling waves lead to a “stable” state of the KE system characterized by a strengthened KE jet and weakened mesoscale eddy variability , as indicated by the enhanced anticyclonic “southern recirculation” locating south of the jet (Fig. ). The strengthened geostrophic KE jet manifests as a meridional dipole in OHC east of Japan (Fig. ). In addition to the PDO, the KE is also modulated by the upstream Kuroshio path south of Japan. The PDO shifted to a positive phase in ~2014, and correspondingly, an unstable dynamical state of the KE was established in early 2017, as indicated by the weakened southern recirculation (Fig. ). However, the Kuroshio large meander occurred in August 2017 - with the Kuroshio following an offshore meandering path in the Shikoku basin south of Japan – compelled the KE to switch back to the stable state in late 2017 and remain stable since then . In contrast, earlier phase shifts of the PDO, such as those in 1992 and 2003, also led to an unstable state of the KE after ~3 years, which persisted for many years (Fig. and Supplementary Fig. ). Albeit interrupted in 2017, there has been a persistently stable KE since ~2009. This led to an overall trend of the KE toward its stable state during the past decades, given the more unstable KE during the 1980s and 1990s due to the positive PDO phase (Supplementary Fig. ). This trend manifests as a dipole-like structure in heat storage east of Japan (Fig. ). By affecting the overlying storm tracks and large-scale winds, the stable KE also contributed to the cooling in the MNP and the warming in the eastern basin .

The response of KE to Rossby waves is successfully reproduced by a 0.1° simulation of HYCOM (Methods; Supplementary Fig. ) that can better resolve the Kuroshio path and mesoscale eddies. As a result, this 0.1° simulation can well represent the heat redistribution driven by PDO winds, including the meridional dipole east of Japan with a lag time of ~4 years (Fig. ) and the cooling in the MNP with a lag time of ~9 years (Fig. ) to the negative peak of the PDO. This ~9-year lag time reflects the slow propagation of upwelling Rossby waves between 40°–52°N (Supplementary Figs. and ) generated by positive w anomalies near the west coasts of North America (Fig. ). In addition, the delayed impact of the KE system also contributes to the ~9 year lag time (Supplementary Fig. ). There are also detailed discrepancies between the 0.1° simulation and observation in the KE region (Fig. compared to Fig. ), which may result from the lack of feedback between mesoscale eddies and the atmosphere in our standard-alone HYCOM simulation. The eddy-atmosphere feedback has been demonstrated to efficiently damp mesoscale eddies and amend the simulated strength and pathway of the KE .

Most CMIP6 models are of horizontal resolutions of ~50 or ~100 km in their ocean components, close to our 0.5° HYCOM simulation. We examine how the PDO-induced heat redistribution is represented in these models. Regressions using individual models (Methods) suggest that CMIP6 can capture some large-scale features of the PDO-induced changes in surface winds and OHC (Fig. and Supplementary Fig. ). The cooling of the MNP is seen at a lag of ~9 years, but the spatial structure does not correlate with the observed regression (Fig. compared to Fig. ). Owing to coarse resolutions, CMIP6 models cannot realistically represent the complex dynamics governing the lagged response to the PDO. The PDO’s signatures in the NWTP are weaker in models than in observations, linked to uncertain tropical Pacific wind changes (Supplementary Fig. ). This indicates a weaker coupling between the PDO and the tropical Pacific climate in models . By prescribing the observed (reanalysis) surface winds, the 0.5° HYCOM simulations can well reproduce the response of NWTP changes associated with the PDO winds (Supplementary Fig. ). Furthermore, the improved fidelity of the 0.1° HYCOM (Fig. ) relative to the 0.5° HYCOM and CMIP6 models highlights the necessity of sufficient model resolutions to account for the complex ocean dynamics involved in heat redistribution.

To further investigate the PDO’s impact, we select 5 model realizations with the simulated PDO showing the highest positive correlations with the observed PDO ( + 5 models) and 5 model realizations showing the highest negative correlation with the observation (-5 models). As such, contrasting the +5 and -5 models mimics the impacts of the observed PDO phase shifts on the OHC trends since 1958 (Supplementary Fig. ). The composite of +5 minus -5 models reassures that the PDO phase shifts in observation can dampen the heat storage in the MNP and enhance the warming of the NWTP (Supplementary Fig. ) through wind-driven heat redistribution (Supplementary Fig. ). Therefore, despite unresolved regional structures due to coarse model resolutions, discrepancies between CMIP6 models and observations in the North Pacific heat storage pattern, particularly the contrasts in the NWTP and the MNP, are primarily explained by natural variability represented by the PDO.

Emergence of anthropogenic heat storage

The above analysis indicates that the emergence of anthropogenic heat storage has been delayed by natural variability in some key regions of the North Pacific and will eventually take place in the future as the emission of greenhouse gases continues. Therefore, it is instructive to estimate the time of emergence (ToE) of anthropogenic heat storage based on CMIP6 simulations (Fig. ), although we fully acknowledge that such estimation is subject to potential influence from inaccurate ocean dynamics as discussed earlier. Here, we primarily utilize the piControl simulation, historical simulation, and projections under three emission scenarios (SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, and SSP5-8.5) of 20 CMIP6 models to determine the “signal-to-noise” ratio and compute the ToE for the 0–2000 m OHC change (Methods ).

Under the “middle-of-the-road” scenario of SSP2-4.5, a scenario viewed as where social and economic trends do not shift markedly from historical patterns, the anthropogenic heat storage (Fig. ) has already emerged (e.g., ToE earlier than the 2020s) in the Southern Oceans between 55°–33°S and the tropical and northern Atlantic. In the North Pacific, the ToE is as early as the 2010s in marginal seas along the basin rim, including the western coasts of North America, the Bering Sea, the Okhotsk Sea (most of which is shallower than 2000 m and not estimated for OHC and ToE), and the Japan Sea (Fig. ). By contrast, anthropogenic signals have not emerged by present (the 2020s) in the basin interior and even until the 2050s in the MNP, KE, and NWTP where we see obvious model-observation discrepancies. The ToEs under the SSP1-2.6 and SSP5-8.5 scenarios are similar in spatial distribution to those under SSP2-4.5 but postponed and advanced in average time (Supplementary Fig. ), respectively. In addition, the signal-to-noise ratios calculated from the observational datasets are generally in line with the ToEs based on CMIP6 simulations in spatial pattern (Supplementary Fig. ), suggesting the reasonableness of the ToE estimation.

In marginal seas of the North Pacific, such as the Bering Sea and the eastern boundary coasts, anthropogenic heat storage has already been emerging. The multi-model median ToE is during the 2010s and 2020s in these regions regardless of the emission scenario (Fig. ). By contrast, the MNP, KE, and NWTP regions show delayed ToEs and relatively high sensitivities to the emission scenario (Fig. ). Under the SSP1-2.6 and SSP2-4.5 scenarios, anthropogenic heat storage is projected to emerge in the latter half of this century in these regions, such as the 2080s in the KE region. Under the SSP5-8.5 scenario, the ToE is as early as the 2040s in the MNP, the 2050s in the KE, and the 2060s in the NWTP. To summarize, despite uncertainties arising from inter-model spread, the North Pacific heat storage is projected to remain under a significant impact of natural variability until the late 21 century, except for marginal seas along the basin rim. Therefore, we expect that the heat storage pattern of the North Pacific will alter but probably still differ from that projected by climate models in the upcoming several decades.

Summary and Implications

Based on observational datasets, ocean model experiments, and CMIP6 model simulations, we demonstrate a strong modification effect by the PDO on the observed heat storage pattern in the North Pacific since the mid-20 century. Specifically, phase shifts of the PDO in recent decades have altered the trends in surface winds over the North Pacific. Surface wind changes induced meridional heat redistribution through Rossby waves and variability of the KE system, effectively erasing the warming in the MNP and fueling the heat pile-up in the NWTP. These effects led to a more complex heat storage pattern in observations than in models by creating regional warming/cooling structures that mask the human-induced fingerprints.

Recognition of the strong influence of the PDO on heat storage provides critical insights into near-term climate prediction when anthropogenic signals have not emerged. The persistent negative phase of PDO since 1998 terminated in the mid-2010s (Fig. ). If the PDO switches to a positive phase in the upcoming decade, the MNP “warming hole” shall vanish soon, and the basin-scale heat storage will evolve toward the pattern projected by models. However, the recent triple La Niña events in 2020–2023 indicate a possibility of a lengthened negative phase, which acts to maintain the existing heat storage pattern. The broad impacts of the PDO on surface atmospheric and ocean conditions have been well established, such as storm tracks, atmospheric rivers, and Pacific salmon production . Here, we add that the PDO is also critical for the transient response of the North Pacific to anthropogenic forcing. Through modulating wind-driven ocean circulations, the PDO fundamentally regulates the distributions of anthropogenic heat, CO , and the Fukushima nuclear effluents in the North Pacific and their spread into the Indian and Arctic Oceans via the Indonesian through-flow and Bering Strait , respectively. These implications highlight the urgent need for an accurate initialized prediction of the PDO and its far-reaching impacts.

Climate models tend to underestimate the PDO variability and its impacts over the historical period . As a result, none of the 20 CMIP6 models has produced a historical heat storage pattern resembling the observation (not shown). Increasing model resolution seems promising. The representation of natural variability in the North Pacific is considerably improved in ~10 km simulations compared to ~100 km simulations ; our analysis based on the 0.1° HYCOM simulation also supports this notion. In addition, increasing ensemble members can better account for the extreme cases of natural variability, such as the 1998–2014 negative phase , and improve the ToE estimate.

The human-induced heat storage pattern in the Pacific Ocean is projected to eventually emerge by the late 21 century, which is much later than in the Atlantic and Southern Oceans. Apart from the strong impacts of natural variability, the postponed ToEs in the Pacific are linked to the lack of a deep-reaching meridional overturning circulation, which also holds true for the Indian Ocean. In the North Atlantic and Southern Oceans, overturning circulations can efficiently communicate anthropogenic heat to the deep ocean, enhancing the basin-average heat uptake and the signal-to-noise ratio in heat storage. Nevertheless, human-induced warming has already been emerging in marginal seas along the North Pacific basin rim, such as the Bering Sea, Okhotsk Sea, Japan Sea, Gulf of Alaska, and western coasts of North America. Increasing frequency and severity of marine heatwaves have been extensively reported recently , with adverse influences on marine ecosystems and regional socioeconomics . Anthropogenic fingerprints in these marginal seas could be enhanced by influence from lands where the air temperature warms quicker than in oceans or coastal processes that are insufficiently resolved by climate models. This calls for extensive investigations to reveal the mechanisms underlying the warming of marginal seas and multi-disciplinary implications, which shall aid the prediction and decision-making.

Methods

Datasets

To estimate the 0–2000 m ocean heat content (OHC) trend since the mid-20 century, ocean temperature data since 1958 from four observational analyses and one reanalysis product are utilized. The four observational analyses are the Institute of Atmospheric Physics ocean analysis (IAP) for 1958–2021 provided by the Chinese Academy of Sciences , the World Ocean Atlas (WOA) for 1958–2018 provided by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration’s (NOAA’s) National Centers for Environmental Information of the United States ; the Ishii analysis for 1958–2021 ; and the version 4.2.2 of the Met Office Hadley Centre “EN” series of datasets (EN4) for 1958–2021 provided by the Met Office of the United Kingdom . The one ocean reanalysis product is the Ocean Reanalysis System 4 (ORA-S4) for 1958–2017 from the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF). All five datasets provide 1° × 1° ocean temperature fields. WOA provides pentad-mean data in annual intervals, and monthly fields of IAP, Ishii, EN4, and ORA-S4 are averaged into annual-mean data. Surface winds of four atmospheric reanalysis products are used, including the 1.25° × 1.25° Japanese 55 year Reanalysis (JRA55) product for 1958–2021 , the 1° × 1° ORA-S4 product as a combination of the 40 year ECMWF Re-Analysis (ERA-40) of 1958-1988 and the ECMWF Interim Re-Analysis (ERA-Interim) of 1989-2017 , the 2.5° x 2.5° National Centers for Environmental Prediction-National Center for Atmospheric Research (NCEP-NCAR) reanalysis for 1958–2021 , and the 0.25° x 0.25° fifth generation ECMWF reanalysis (ERA5) for 1958–2021 . To examine the changes in the Kuroshio Extension (KE) jet, the 0.25° x 0.25° sea surface height data distributed by the Copernicus Marine and Environment Monitoring Service (CMEMS) for 1993–2021 is used.

The coupled model simulations

Monthly outputs of the pre-industrial control (piControl) (the last 100 years), historical (from 1958–2014), Shared Socioeconomic Pathways 126 (SSP1-2.6), SSP2-4.5, and SSP5-8.5 (from 2015–2100) simulations of twenty models of the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6 (CMIP6) are analyzed. These models include the following: ACCESS-CM2, ACCESS-ESM1-5, BCC-CSM2-MR, CAMS-CSM1-0, CESM2-WACCM, CNRM-CM6-1, CanESM5, EC-Earth3, EC-Earth3-Veg, FGOALS-g3, FIO-ESM-2-0, GFDL-ESM4, GISS-E2-1-G, INM-CM4-8, INM-CM5-0, MPI-ESM1-2-LR, MRI-ESM2-0, NESM3, NorESM2-LM, NorESM2-MM. All CMIP6 models integrate for at least 500 years under the pre-industrial CO level (piControl) and then from 1850–2014 to form the historical simulation. In the historical simulation, all models are forced by the observed external forcing (such as CO , aerosols, and ozone) . Owing to the nonlinearity of the climate system, the phase of internal variability (such as El Niño-Southern Oscillation and PDO) is different across models and different from observation. Therefore, anomalies arising from internal variability cancel each other when we average these 20 models to obtain the multi-model mean (MMM). As such, CMIP6 MMM primarily represents the externally (mainly anthropogenic) forced change, and the inter-model spread represents internal variability and differences in model physics. Selection of these 20 models is dictated by data availability for the three emission scenarios when our work begins. Monthly fields of CMIP6 simulations are interpolated onto the same set of 1° × 1° grids and averaged into annual-mean data to match observational datasets.

HYCOM experiments

We use the Hybrid Coordinate Ocean Model (HYCOM) version-2.3.01 to simulate and understand the ocean heat storage pattern. The HYCOM 0.5° simulation adopts a quasi-global ocean domain (78°S-75°N, 30°-389.5°E) with resolutions of 0.5° × 0.5° × 50 levels. The model is spun up from a state of rest for 600 years under the daily JRA55 atmospheric forcing of 1958, and the atmospheric surface forcing fields include the wind stress, net shortwave and longwave radiations (SWR and LWR), wind speed, precipitation, and air temperature and humidity. The control run (CTRL) is forced with realistic daily atmospheric forcings of JRA55 reanalysis from 1958 to 2019. CTRL contains complete physical processes of forming ocean heat storage pattern and is compared with observational data to evaluate the model performance. Two sensitivity runs are conducted to explore the effects of different drivers on the ocean heat storage pattern. Only wind stress adopts realistic daily fields in wind stress run (WND), while all the other forcings, such as wind speed and radiations, are fixed to the 1958 fields as in spin-up. In the WND, OHC trends primarily arise from heat redistribution due to wind stress-driven changes in ocean circulation processes. The heat flux run (HTFL) only adopts realistic SWR and LWR, 2 m air temperature, specific humidity, and wind speed for 1958–2019 but fixes wind stress and precipitation to the 1958 fields. As such, changes in these fields of HTFL affect OHC trends mainly by altering the heat fluxes (SWR, LWR, sensible, and latent heat fluxes), representing the role of heat-driven processes or ocean heat uptake. This linear separation of the wind and heat flux forcing effects inevitably leads to nonlinear interactions or model errors, but HTFL and WND still provide useful insights into the underlying physical processes. To examine the impacts of decadally varying KE jet and its associated mesoscale eddies, we also conducted HYCOM 0.1° simulation using the same domain as the HYCOM 0.5° simulation but with resolutions of 0.1° × 0.1° × 50 levels. After a 20-year spin-up, the HYCOM 0.1° simulation is integrated forwards from 1979 to 2021 under hourly ERA5 atmospheric forcing. The comparison of the HYCOM 0.1° simulation and the HYCOM 0.5° CTRL allows us to test the possible impact of mesoscale oceanic processes on heat storage pattern in the North Pacific.

Time of Emergence (ToE)

A “signal-to-noise” method is adopted to compute the time of emergence (ToE) of the OHC trend. We use the piControl simulation, historical simulation, and projections under three emission scenarios (SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, and SSP5-8.5) of 20 CMIP6 models to determine the “signal-to-noise” ratio (SNR). The noise is defined as the interannual standard deviation of the quadratically detrended OHC variability of piControl simulation in the last 100 years, representing the natural noise envelope. The anthropogenic signals for 1958–2100 are computed as the OHC differences between the historical plus three emission scenario simulations and the time-mean piControl value. The ToE is defined as the time when the signal last exceeds and remains above a threshold of two times of noise (i.e., SNR > 2.0). The threshold for SNR is chosen as 2.0 because it represents a 95% confidence level of signal emergence . For the regional ToEs, the noise and signal of selected regions are first averaged and then the time with SNR > 2.0 is searched. We also estimated the observed SNR to examine the possible spatial pattern of the observed anthropogenic OHC, as using observational data to calculate the ToE is infeasible, which relies on pre-industrial simulations from climate models. The signal is defined as the observed OHC trend (change) for 1958–2021, with the noise being one standard deviation of the detrended OHC.
